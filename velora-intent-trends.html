The number isn't the trend.

The hospital's records show the latest value. Velora shows what direction it's been moving. Same data; different question.

Each individual reading was reviewed. Each individual note read "above target, intensify". Nobody saw the year. The doctor in front of the patient today is the first person in twelve months in a position to read the trajectory — and only because Velora is the one who plotted it.

The case

Anita Mehta, 47, T2DM & HTN — twelve months, five readings

Anita has been a patient at this hospital for eight years. She has T2DM since 2018 and HTN since 2020. Her HbA1c was reviewed at every visit. Each reviewer wrote a sound clinical note. No reviewer saw the trajectory. Each reading drifted half a percent. Across a year, half-percents add up.

What was written, visit-by-visit
Every note:
  • "Above target. Continue current Rx."
  • "Above target. Intensify lifestyle."
  • "Above target. Reinforce adherence."

Each note was correct. The trajectory across all five visits — a steady +1.8% over twelve months — was correct too. The patient and the doctor did not have it as a single number until Velora computed it.

"The trend is unambiguous. HbA1c rising 1.8 percentage points over four quarters across two prescribers. The case for therapy escalation rests on the trajectory, not the latest value alone." — Endocrinology consult note, 7 May 2026

What you're watching

Stack 1 is the five raw HbA1c readings, plus the six BP readings. Each row carries the date, the ordering doctor, and the specialty — and clicks open to the source lab note in the hospital's records.

Stack 2 layers two computations on top: direction (deterministic arithmetic on the raw values — slope, delta over window) and target (from the guideline body the hospital config picked). The card shows both ADA 2024 (general) and ICMR 2018 (Indian context) so the doctor sees which target is in play.

Defence: trend direction is deterministic. Targets are from a named guideline body. Velora does not say "intensify therapy" — it shows the trajectory and the target, and the doctor decides.

How the drawer is structured

Two stacks. Two epistemic levels. Visually separated.

For Trends, Stack 1 is the raw values — a table of readings with dates and prescribers. Stack 2 is the computed direction and the cited target. Each lives in its own container so the doctor never confuses an arithmetic line with a clinical recommendation.

Stack 1 Hospital records Fact

Raw readings, attributed and verifiable. No AI authoring.

Click any row to open the source measurement in the hospital's records.

  • HbA1c · BP · eGFR · LDL · BMI · EF · KT/V · weight
  • Each reading: value, date, ordering doctor, specialty
  • Reference range visible per metric
  • Up to 6 most-recent readings per metric in the drawer
Defence: every reading traces to a single measurement_id. Click → open source. Reproducible.
Stack 2 AI Interpretation · informational only Suggested

Trajectory + cited guideline target. No clinical recommendation.

Per metric, two things: direction (deterministic) and target (from the named guideline body the hospital config picked).

  • Direction: rising / falling / stable · slope over window
  • Delta: signed change vs N months prior
  • Status: at-target / above / below — a comparison, not a verdict
  • Cited section: e.g., ADA 2024 §6.4 · ICMR 2018 §4.2 · WHO HEARTS 2023
Defence: direction is computed from raw values (reproducible). Targets are from the guideline body and section the hospital config named. Velora does not author what the trend means clinically.

Stack 1 is always above Stack 2. A doctor with 5 seconds reads only Stack 1. A doctor with 15 seconds reads both.

Same patient · different hospital

Same trend. Different hospital. Different target wins.

For HbA1c alone, three guideline bodies cover the same clinical area: ADA (general, US-led), ICMR (Indian-population, stricter), and NICE (UK). The same value is "above target" against ICMR and "at target" against ADA's older-adult cut-off. Velora's job is to show which target is in play and where it comes from.
