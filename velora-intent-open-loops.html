Velora · Open loops

Every order starts a chain.
Velora reads what closed.

A lab to be reviewed. A referral to be acted on. A follow-up to attend. A prescription to fill. The hospital records the start. Velora reads the finish.

In a busy multi-specialty hospital, things drop silently — not from clinical error, but from the absence of a single view that watches every chain end-to-end. The data to know what didn't close is already in the hospital's records. Nobody pulls it.

It is a librarian, not a clinician. It tracks loops; it does not prescribe action.

The case

Lakshmi Pandey, 64, T2DM with neuropathy

Four open chains across three months. Each chart shows the order. None shows the closure. Lakshmi is on tomorrow's GenMed list. Today's chart-review tool will show the most recent visit. It will not tell Dr. Pandey that a referral she sent in February to Cardiology never resulted in a visit, or that the fasting glucose she ordered last month never came back, or that the statin she started in January has not been refilled since.

General medicine
Started in this department, never closed:
  • Statin (atorvastatin 20 mg) prescribed 18 Jan
  • Fasting glucose ordered 22 Feb · no result on file
  • Last clinical contact 10 Mar · marked no-show
Endocrinology
Acted in this department, never closed:
  • Referral sent to Cardiology 12 Feb (atypical chest pain)
  • HbA1c result returned 15 Feb · never reviewed in note
  • Follow-up booked 10 Mar · same no-show as above
Pharmacy & ops
Operationally:
  • Atorvastatin dispensed once 18 Jan · no refill
  • Last in-hospital fill: 110 days ago
  • Cardiology has no scheduled appointment for this patient

Each system was clinically and operationally complete inside its own boundary. The four loops are visible only when the four systems are read together. The hospital's existing tools did not surface that until the next admission.

"The cardiology referral never resulted in a consult. The fasting glucose was never reviewed. The follow-up was missed and never rebooked. The statin lapsed at the in-hospital pharmacy. None of these alone is unusual; together they are why she came back to ER." — Discharge summary, 28 April 2026
How the drawer presents this case

Watch Velora read Lakshmi's open loops.

Stack 1 surfaces the four open chains as raw operational facts — what was started, when, by whom, and how long it has been open. Stack 2 layers the hospital's signed-off thresholds on top, marking each loop as OPEN, STALE, or UNCLOSED. The thresholds are operational config — not clinical guidelines — and the hospital signs them off before pilot launch.

How the drawer is structured

Two stacks. Two epistemic levels. Visually separated.

For Open loops, Stack 1 is the chain itself — order placed, status of its closure. Stack 2 is the operational rule — when the hospital considers a chain stale. They live in different containers because they answer different questions: "what happened" vs "what counts as too long".

Stack 1 Hospital records Fact

The chain itself, attributed and verifiable. No AI authoring.

Click any card to open the source order, referral, appointment, or pharmacy entry in the hospital's records.

  • Lab orders without a linked result_id
  • Referrals without a destination-department visit
  • Appointments with status = no-show or cancelled-without-rebook
  • Prescriptions without an in-hospital pharmacy fill in N days
Defence: every line traces to a single structured event id. Click → open the source row in the hospital's records. Reproducible.
Stack 2 AI Interpretation · informational only Suggested

Operational thresholds applied to each chain. No clinical guideline authoring.

The thresholds are signed off by the hospital before pilot. They are operational, not clinical.

  • Lab loop: open > N days → OPEN
  • Referral loop: no destination visit > N days → STALE
  • Follow-up loop: no-show, no rebook > N days → UNCLOSED
  • Prescription loop: no in-hospital fill > N days → SIGNAL (with disclosure)
Defence: the rule is the hospital's signed operational config, versioned, with the signing clinician. Velora does not claim any delay is harmful. It claims this loop is open under your config.

Stack 1 is always above Stack 2. A doctor with 5 seconds reads only Stack 1. A doctor with 15 seconds reads both.

Same chain · different hospital

Same loop. Different hospital. Different threshold.

There is no clinical guideline that says "a fasting glucose is stale at 7 days". That is a workflow choice the hospital makes — based on its OPD volume, its lab turnaround, its referral patterns. Two hospitals can each be correct and pick different numbers.

Velora's job is to apply this hospital's signed-off config and surface the loops that breach it. Same code at Medanta and Mayo. Different thresholds.
