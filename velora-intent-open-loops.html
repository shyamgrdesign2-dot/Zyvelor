The case

Dr. Anjali Pandey · Friday end-of-shift · 17:40

Dr. Pandey's panel has 142 active patients. Across this past month she has ordered 38 labs, sent 9 referrals, booked 22 follow-ups, prescribed 51 in-hospital scripts. Most are closed. 11 chains across 8 patients haven't. The patient charts can't tell her this — each one shows that this patient has one open lab. Velora reads across all 142 panels, groups by chain type, and sorts by age. The doctor's own inbox.

A doctor's queue, not a patient's chart. The chart-level view shows one open chain at a time and asks the doctor to remember the rest. The queue-level view sorts every unfinished item the doctor started, across every patient they've touched, by age. Five minutes a week, end of shift.

"Velora's queue caught two referrals I'd lost track of and a fasting glucose I'd forgotten I ordered. The fasting glucose was on Lakshmi — who came back to ER three weeks later. If I had read the queue then, I would have called her." — Dr. Pandey · pilot week 5
How the queue surface looks

11 chains. 8 patients. One worklist sorted by age.

My open loops is a doctor-centric surface — the doctor's own queue across every patient they've touched, not a per-patient drawer. Each chain is a structured event with a stable id; the status pill (OPEN · STALE · UNCLOSED · SIGNAL) is computed against the hospital's signed operational thresholds. Five minutes a week, end of shift.

Same chain · different hospital

Same loop. Different hospital. Different threshold.

There is no clinical guideline that says "a fasting glucose is stale at 7 days". That is a workflow choice the hospital makes — based on its OPD volume, its lab turnaround, its referral patterns. Two hospitals can each be correct and pick different numbers.

Velora's job is to apply this hospital's signed-off config and surface the loops that breach it. Same code at Medanta and Mayo. Different thresholds.
