Don't write blind.

The case

Suresh Iyer, 62, post-MI · about to be prescribed an NSAID

Suresh walked into General Medicine OPD complaining of joint pain. He had a heart attack 18 days ago. Five active medications, three specialties, one allergy on file. Dr. Mehta is reaching for ibuprofen 400 mg TDS. The cardiologist's note from two weeks ago is in another tab. The allergy entry from 2018 is in a third. The lipid panel from 8 days ago is in a fourth.

Cardiology
Active, prescribed in last 18 days:
  • Apixaban 5 mg BD · started 24 Apr
  • Bisoprolol 5 mg OD · started 24 Apr
  • Atorvastatin 80 mg HS · started 12 Apr
Endocrinology
Active, longstanding:
  • Metformin 1 g BD · since 2024
  • Empagliflozin 10 mg OD · since Aug 2025
  • Last HbA1c 7.2 % · 12 Mar
General medicine (today)
About to write:
  • Ibuprofen 400 mg TDS for joint pain
  • Allergy on file: Ibuprofen — rash, 2018
  • Lipid panel done 26 Apr · LDL 88

Three independently sound charts. The risk is at the intersection — and the intersection is what the doctor has to stitch in their head, in three minutes, between two patients. Velora reads it instead.

"Class C interaction. Apixaban + ibuprofen materially raises bleed risk in the post-MI window. Patient also has a documented ibuprofen rash from 2018. The interaction would have been caught at the pharmacy. The allergy might not have been." — Pharmacy review note, 8 May 2026

What you're watching

Stack 1 is the full medication and allergy picture, attributed to the prescribing specialty and start date. The doctor reads it like a chart — verbatim, no interpretation.

Stack 2 layers four rule-based checks: a DDI flag (Lexicomp), an allergy match (chart entry), a guideline-anchored statin status (ADA), and a duplicate-order hint. Each rule cites its source.

Defence: Velora does not approve a dose, recommend a substitute, or stop a prescription. It surfaces structured rules from sources the hospital signed off on. The doctor decides.
Same patient · different hospital

Same patient. Different hospital. Different DDI source wins.

Different hospitals license different DDI references — Lexicomp, Micromedex, Mayo Drug Reference, hospital pharmacy formulary — and each has a slightly different rule set, severity grading, and class definition. Velora's engine is the same; the source the engine consults is configurable per hospital.
