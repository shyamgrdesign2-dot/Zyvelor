The case

Dr. Anjali Pandey · GenMed · 30 minutes between rounds

It's 11:30. Dr. Pandey has finished morning OPD, has 30 minutes before her ward round. She wants to scan, across all 142 patients she's seen recently, where an action she or a colleague started hasn't closed. Not "patients we've never seen" — there's no recall infrastructure here, and Indian OPD doesn't work that way. Care Gaps is sharper: an event happened, an expected next step was implied, and it hasn't happened yet. Each gap traces to a structured id.

Discharge follow-up missing
Discharged in last 30 d, no follow-up booked:
  • 4 patients · ages 14–28 d
  • Anchor: discharge_id · expected: scheduled OPD
  • Action: book a follow-up via OPD desk
Lab returned, unread
Result drawn, no clinical note attached:
  • 5 patients · oldest 5 d (K+ 5.9)
  • Anchor: result_id · expected: clinical note
  • Action: review and document
Referral never landed
Referral sent, no destination visit:
  • 6 patients · oldest 85 d
  • Anchor: referral_id · expected: destination_visit_id
  • Action: re-send or call the destination dept
Drug-target mismatch
On therapy, last lab outside target:
  • 8 patients · metformin + HbA1c > 8
  • Anchor: rx_id + result_id · target: ICMR
  • Action: review at next encounter

Every gap is a join between two existing rows — an event that happened (admission, lab, referral, prescription) and an expected next-step row that's missing. Cypher checks the join. No gap depends on data that doesn't exist. No gap depends on a patient the hospital has never seen.

"I had thirty minutes between rounds. Care Gaps showed me four discharges with no follow-up booked from my unit, and a stale referral I'd sent two months ago. I called the OPD desk and the cardiology lead. Closed five things in twenty minutes." — Dr. Pandey · pilot week 4 · 8 May 2026

Each row carries a structured anchor (discharge_id, result_id, referral_id, rx_id) and the expected next-step that's missing. Click any patient to open the MDT brief.

Same event · different hospital

Same event. Different hospital. Different threshold wins.

A K+ of 5.9 might be tier-warning at one hospital and tier-critical at another, depending on the lab's signed reference range. An admission in the last 30 days might or might not flag depending on the hospital's operational window. Velora's job is to apply this hospital's signed thresholds and cite which rule fired.
